Why Is Super Visa Insurance Being Denied?

A Super Visa denial happens when your insurance provider refuses to pay out on a claim you've submitted. This can feel devastating, especially when your loved ones need medical care. But insurance companies don't deny claims randomly—there are specific reasons.

The most common reasons for Super Visa insurance denials include:

  • Pre-existing conditions not disclosed: If your parent has diabetes, heart disease, or other existing health conditions that weren't reported during the application, insurers may deny coverage.
  • Treatment received before the policy started: Medical care obtained before your insurance policy became active isn't covered.
  • Visiting a non-hospital emergency room: Some policies only cover care at accredited hospitals or approved facilities.
  • Non-emergency procedures or cosmetic treatments: Super Visa insurance covers emergencies, not routine check-ups or elective procedures.
  • Traveling against medical advice: If your parent was advised not to travel by their doctor, the claim may be denied.
  • Incomplete or inaccurate application information: Even small errors or omissions can lead to denials.

According to Immigration, Refugees and Citizenship Canada (IRCC), Super Visa insurance must provide a minimum of CAD $100,000 in emergency medical coverage. If your policy doesn't meet this requirement, your entire visa application could be rejected.

What Happens to Your Money When a Claim Is Denied?

Here's where it gets confusing for many families: when your claim is denied, your insurance premiums don't automatically get refunded. You've already paid for the coverage—the premium is non-refundable in most cases, even if no claims are made or if claims are denied.

However, there's an important distinction:

  • Premium refunds: Generally not available once the policy is in effect, unless you cancel within a cooling-off period (usually 10-14 days).
  • Claim reimbursements: These should be paid if your claim is valid. A denial means the insurer won't reimburse you for medical expenses.
  • Appeal rights: You have the right to appeal a denied claim within 30-90 days, depending on your policy.

How to Challenge a Super Visa Insurance Denial

If your claim has been denied, don't give up. You have options.

Step 1: Request a detailed explanation. Contact your insurer and ask for a written explanation of why your claim was denied. They must provide specific reasons.

Step 2: Gather supporting documents. Collect medical records, receipts, prescriptions, and any other evidence supporting your claim. If there was a miscommunication about pre-existing conditions, gather medical history documentation.

Step 3: File a formal appeal. Submit your appeal in writing within the timeframe specified in your policy (usually 30-90 days). Include all supporting documentation.

Step 5: Escalate if necessary. If the appeal is denied, you can file a complaint with your provincial insurance regulator (such as Manitoba Public Insurance or provincial insurance boards).

How to Prevent Super Visa Insurance Denials

The best strategy is prevention. Here's how to protect your family:

  • Full disclosure: Report all pre-existing conditions during the application, even if they seem minor. Honesty prevents future denials.
  • Read the fine print: Understand exactly what your policy covers and doesn't cover.
  • Choose the right coverage amount: Don't just meet the minimum CAD $100,000—consider higher limits for better protection.
  • Work with a licensed broker: An expert can explain coverage details and help you avoid costly mistakes.
  • Keep all documentation: Maintain copies of your policy, receipts, medical records, and correspondence with your insurer.
